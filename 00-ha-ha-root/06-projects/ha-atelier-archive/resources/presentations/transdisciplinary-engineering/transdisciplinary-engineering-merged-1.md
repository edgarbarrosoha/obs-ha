**What is TE?**

---

**TRANSDISCIPLINARY ENGINEERING** 

  

Today’s complex problems, especially those that have a large impact on the environment and society, require a holistic approach to solving them by considering knowledge from a diverse range of disciplines, technical as well as social, and from user and practitioner communities. Interchange of such cross-disciplinary knowledge is needed to determine the overall impact on the environment and society. Many examples exist of engineering innovations that have a large adverse effect on the environment by not considering measures and regulations for mitigating these effects, for example, non-biodegradable plastics, certain pesticides and fertilizing products, use of greenhouse gasses, the long-term effect of medication and medical procedures, etc.

  

To create engineering solutions that minimize environmental and societal damage, engineering design and practices must involve people with the necessary knowledge and methods to determine the impact of new products and services, not only on client and user satisfaction but also on long-term sustainability. Designers and engineers need to come from a wide range of disciplines to achieve such sustainable solutions. The support of advanced ICT technology is deemed indispensable.

  

[The International Society of Transdisciplinary Engineering (ISTE)](https://intsoctransde.org/) aims to create awareness that engineering practices need to evolve into transdisciplinary engineering practices in which the exchange of different types of knowledge and people from a diverse range of disciplines is fundamental.

  

Transdisciplinary Engineering (TE) is the exchange of knowledge in the context of innovation, in product, process, organization, or social environment. Through ISTE, the exchange of knowledge is possible in its annual conferences, the TE Conferences, and through the publication of special issues in scientific journals.

# Transdisciplinary Government Planning in Practice: The Guanajuato Case Study

## A Real-World Application of Horizons Architecture for Complex Endeavors

Distinguished colleagues, I want to share with you how the theoretical framework of Horizons Architecture was applied to one of the most complex challenges in public administration: developing a comprehensive government program that truly serves diverse stakeholders while maintaining strategic coherence across a six-year mandate.

## The Challenge: Beyond Traditional Government Planning

The state of Guanajuato faced a familiar yet complex challenge: how to develop knowledge and practices that promote what is perceived to be the common good while taking into account the complexity of problems and the diversity of perceptions. Traditional government planning often falls into what we might call "siloed thinking" - where each department operates independently, citizen input is tokenistic, and long-term vision gets lost in short-term political cycles.

Our transdisciplinary approach needed to grasp the complexity of problems, take into account the diversity of life-world and scientific perceptions of problems, link abstract and case-specific knowledge, and develop knowledge and practices that promote what is perceived to be the common good.

## Phase 1: Contextual Foundation (HA's Context & Legacy Dimensions)

### Building the Knowledge Base

The first phase exemplified what we call in HA the **Context** and **Legacy** dimensions working in tandem. We began by systematically collecting and analyzing socioeconomic data, key indicators, and previous government programs. This wasn't mere data gathering - it was what transdisciplinary research calls systems knowledge: questions about the genesis and possible development of a problem and about interpretations of the problem in the life-world.

### AI-Enhanced Pattern Recognition

Using artificial intelligence for national and international trend analysis, we demonstrated how AI agents within the GAO can aid in simulation and scenario analysis, processing vast amounts of external contextual data that would be impossible for human teams alone to synthesize effectively.

### Establishing Historical Continuity

The evaluation of previous program traceability and the alignment with the State Development Plan (PED) 2050 embodied HA's emphasis on integrating historical data analysis, real-time situational awareness, and scenario-based foresight into a unified temporal perspective.

## Phase 2: Formal Launch (HA's Community & Legacy Dimensions)

### Stakeholder Network Mapping

The formal launch phase centered on what HA calls the **Community** dimension - the network of human actors, institutional agents, their relationships, roles, values, social dynamics, and coordination needs within the endeavor. Through interviews with the Governor and key actors, we weren't just collecting opinions; we were mapping the complex stakeholder ecosystem that would ultimately determine the program's success.

### Vision Co-Creation

The collaborative workshops with department coordinators demonstrated participatory research involving non-scientific groups and research that crosses the barriers between disciplines. Each department contributed their specialized knowledge while working toward a shared understanding of the state's priorities.

## Phase 3: Social Consultation (HA's Community & Learning Dimensions)

### Multi-Modal Participation Design

This phase exemplified transdisciplinary engineering's core principle: the exchange of knowledge in the context of innovation, in product, process, organization, or social environment. We designed multiple consultation mechanisms:

- Expert roundtables by thematic areas
- Regional workshops with different population groups
- Online consultations with educational communities
- Social media engagement
- Sessions in community centers

### AI-Powered Pattern Recognition

The use of Natural Language Processing to analyze consultation results demonstrated how GAO agents can assist in mining historical data for patterns and insights, processing thousands of citizen inputs to identify key themes and priorities that would be impossible to synthesize manually.

### Closing the Participation Loop

The formal establishment of the State Planning Council (COPLADEG) created what HA calls a governance structure that ensures humans retain ultimate strategic control, ethical judgment, and interpretive authority while leveraging AI capabilities for enhanced analysis.

## Phase 4: Strategic Integration (HA's Learning & Technology Dimensions)

### Data Synthesis and AI-Assisted Prioritization

This phase demonstrated HA's **Technology** dimension in action - the tools, platforms, data systems, infrastructures, methods, and engineering solutions that enable, shape, or accelerate the endeavor. AI tools helped process vast volumes of data, identify patterns, and assist in prioritizing objectives, strategies, and actions.

### Indicator Development and Historical Analysis

The collaborative design of performance indicators with departments, supported by AI tools for analyzing historical indicator trajectories, exemplified the interplay between past, present, and future that characterizes non-linear temporal coordination in complex endeavors.

### Institutional Validation

The peer validation sessions between departments demonstrated what transdisciplinary research calls open encounters: perceiving one's own perspective as only one among several others, and accepting other views as potentially just as relevant as one's own.

## Phase 5: Integration and Dissemination (HA's Projects & Community Dimensions)

### Multi-Format Accessibility

The development of multiple versions - executive summary, infographic version, presentations, and adapted versions for children, people with disabilities, and indigenous communities - embodied the transdisciplinary principle of tailoring results for the target groups.

### AI Agent "Esperanza"

The development of an AI chatbot to facilitate content search within the program demonstrated HA's vision of AI agents acting as adaptive partners within the HA structure, tailored to specific dimensional needs.

## Transdisciplinary Insights and Lessons Learned

### 1. Complexity Requires Integration, Not Just Coordination

This project validated HA's hypothesis that effectively navigating modern challenges requires frameworks that manage these endeavors within their systemic context. Traditional planning approaches would have treated each consultation mechanism or department input as separate streams. Our transdisciplinary approach treated them as interconnected elements of a complex adaptive system.

### 2. AI as Cognitive Partner, Not Replacement

The successful integration of AI throughout the process demonstrated what we call "hybrid intelligence" - using AI to enhance human cognitive functions rather than full automation, particularly for complex, ill-defined problems requiring judgment, creativity, ethical reasoning, and contextual understanding.

### 3. Temporal Coordination is Critical

The alignment of campaign proposals with the 2050 State Development Plan, while incorporating citizen input and departmental priorities, exemplified the core function of temporal coordination: to dynamically link temporal perspectives so that decisions made today are informed not only by immediate operational needs but also by lessons learned and alignment with long-term goals.

### 4. Fractal Scaling in Practice

The process naturally exhibited fractal properties - the same methodology applied at the state level was replicated at department levels, regional levels, and thematic areas, maintaining structural coherence while adapting to diverse contexts and application scales.

## Implications for Transdisciplinary Engineering

This case study demonstrates that transdisciplinary approaches to complex societal challenges are not just theoretical ideals but practical necessities. As the conference theme states, we need "collaborative teams of people with the ability to see a holistic picture, equipped with knowledge and skills in various disciplines, to effectively exploit technologies in this changing and connected world".

The Guanajuato experience shows how frameworks like Horizons Architecture can operationalize the principles of transdisciplinary research, providing structure without rigidity, enabling AI augmentation without losing human agency, and maintaining strategic coherence while embracing participatory complexity.

As we face increasingly complex global challenges - from climate change to social inequality to technological disruption - we need approaches that can handle what contemporary global challenges crossing climate change, pandemics, financial volatility, and large-scale technological transitions manifest as complex adaptive systems characterized by intricate interdependencies, nonlinear dynamics, and emergent phenomena.

The future of transdisciplinary engineering lies not just in crossing disciplinary boundaries, but in creating integrated frameworks that can orchestrate human creativity, AI capabilities, and systemic thinking toward solutions that truly serve the common good.

# Implementing Transdisciplinary Frameworks in Complex Governance:

## The Guanajuato Government Program as a Case Study in Applied Systems Integration

### Abstract

This presentation examines the application of transdisciplinary research principles in developing a comprehensive government program for Guanajuato, Mexico (2024-2030). The case demonstrates how complex, multi-stakeholder endeavors can benefit from systematic integration of diverse knowledge forms, participatory methodologies, and adaptive coordination mechanisms. **What is TE?**

---

**TRANSDISCIPLINARY ENGINEERING** 

  

Today’s complex problems, especially those that have a large impact on the environment and society, require a holistic approach to solving them by considering knowledge from a diverse range of disciplines, technical as well as social, and from user and practitioner communities. Interchange of such cross-disciplinary knowledge is needed to determine the overall impact on the environment and society. Many examples exist of engineering innovations that have a large adverse effect on the environment by not considering measures and regulations for mitigating these effects, for example, non-biodegradable plastics, certain pesticides and fertilizing products, use of greenhouse gasses, the long-term effect of medication and medical procedures, etc.

  

To create engineering solutions that minimize environmental and societal damage, engineering design and practices must involve people with the necessary knowledge and methods to determine the impact of new products and services, not only on client and user satisfaction but also on long-term sustainability. Designers and engineers need to come from a wide range of disciplines to achieve such sustainable solutions. The support of advanced ICT technology is deemed indispensable.

  

[The International Society of Transdisciplinary Engineering (ISTE)](https://intsoctransde.org/) aims to create awareness that engineering practices need to evolve into transdisciplinary engineering practices in which the exchange of different types of knowledge and people from a diverse range of disciplines is fundamental.

  

Transdisciplinary Engineering (TE) is the exchange of knowledge in the context of innovation, in product, process, organization, or social environment. Through ISTE, the exchange of knowledge is possible in its annual conferences, the TE Conferences, and through the publication of special issues in scientific journals.

### What is TE

**TRANSDISCIPLINARY ENGINEERING**   

Today’s complex problems, especially those that have a large impact on the environment and society, require a holistic approach to solving them by considering knowledge from a diverse range of disciplines, technical as well as social, and from user and practitioner communities. Interchange of such cross-disciplinary knowledge is needed to determine the overall impact on the environment and society. Many examples exist of engineering innovations that have a large adverse effect on the environment by not considering measures and regulations for mitigating these effects, for example, non-biodegradable plastics, certain pesticides and fertilizing products, use of greenhouse gasses, the long-term effect of medication and medical procedures, etc.

To create engineering solutions that minimize environmental and societal damage, engineering design and practices must involve people with the necessary knowledge and methods to determine the impact of new products and services, not only on client and user satisfaction but also on long-term sustainability. Designers and engineers need to come from a wide range of disciplines to achieve such sustainable solutions. The support of advanced ICT technology is deemed indispensable.

[The International Society of Transdisciplinary Engineering (ISTE)](https://intsoctransde.org/) aims to create awareness that engineering practices need to evolve into transdisciplinary engineering practices in which the exchange of different types of knowledge and people from a diverse range of disciplines is fundamental.  

Transdisciplinary Engineering (TE) is the exchange of knowledge in the context of innovation, in product, process, organization, or social environment. Through ISTE, the exchange of knowledge is possible in its annual conferences, the TE Conferences, and through the publication of special issues in scientific journals.

**Presenter:**
"Good morning. Thank you for the opportunity to be here.

We are all in the business of solving complex problems. Whether it's climate change, urban renewal, or technological transition, we face challenges characterized by deep uncertainty, countless interdependencies, and a tangled web of stakeholders.

These are not engineering problems in the traditional sense. They are *transdisciplinary* problems. They demand that we not only work across disciplines but that we co-create new methodologies, a shared language to bridge our different worlds. Linear plans fail. Siloed expertise falls short.

Today, I want to introduce a framework designed for precisely this reality: **Horizons Architecture (HA)**. And I won't just talk about theory. I will show you how it was applied to one of the most complex endeavors imaginable: building a strategic government program for six million people, for the next six years, based on the input of tens of thousands of citizens."

---

#### **Slide 2: What is Horizons Architecture (HA)?**
**(A clean, high-level diagram showing the six dimensions of HA: Legacy, Community, Learning, Technology, Context, and Projects, interconnected in a web.)**

**Presenter:**
"So, what is Horizons Architecture?

At its core, HA is a theoretical framework for scaling Human-AI collaboration. It integrates complexity science, systems thinking, and hybrid intelligence to navigate the gap between near-term operational demands and long-range transformational goals.

It’s built on a simple but powerful idea: that any complex endeavor, no matter its scale, can be understood and managed through **six core, interacting dimensions**:

1.  **Legacy:** This is the *'Why'*. The enduring purpose, the mission, the long-term value we aim to create.
2.  **Community:** The *'Who'*. The network of stakeholders, their relationships, values, and power dynamics.
3.  **Context:** The *'What's Happening'*. The external environment—socio-political, economic, environmental—that we operate within.
4.  **Learning:** The *'How We Adapt'*. The process of knowledge acquisition, feedback integration, and capacity building for both humans and AI.
5.  **Technology:** The *'Tools'*. The infrastructure, data systems, and AI capabilities that enable our work.
6.  **Projects:** The *'What We Do'*. The concrete, actionable initiatives and tasks that bring the Legacy to life.

These dimensions are not a linear checklist. They are a dynamic, interconnected system. A change in Context requires new Learning. A new Technology impacts the Community. HA provides the structure to see and manage these interdependencies."

---

#### **Slide 3: The Use Case: Guanajuato Government Program 2024-2030**
**(Images from Guanajuato, photos of workshops, people participating in consultations.)**

**Presenter:**
"Now, let's make this tangible. The government of Guanajuato, Mexico, faced the monumental task of creating its 2024-2030 Government Program. This involved synthesizing campaign promises, aligning with a 2050 state development plan, and, most importantly, conducting a massive social consultation to integrate the voice of the citizens.

It was a perfect storm of transdisciplinary complexity. They needed to integrate:
*   **Social & Political Science:** Through stakeholder engagement and public consultation.
*   **Data Science:** To analyze vast amounts of structured and unstructured data.
*   **Public Administration:** To define strategic goals and performance indicators.
*   And **Systems Engineering:** To orchestrate the entire process without it descending into chaos.

They used the principles of Horizons Architecture to structure this endeavor. Let me walk you through how the activities mapped directly onto the HA framework."

---

#### **Slide 4: Mapping the Process to HA - Phase 1 & 2**
**(A diagram showing the HA dimensions. As the presenter speaks, the corresponding activities from the list appear next to the relevant dimension.)**

**Presenter:**
"The process began with **Phase 1: Contextualization**.

*   Analyzing previous government programs was a **Learning** activity, drawing from the past.
*   Analyzing national and international trends populated the **Context** dimension.
*   And aligning with the State's 2050 plan was a foundational act of defining the **Legacy**.

In **Phase 2: Formal Start**, the focus shifted to the people.

*   Interviews with the Governor and key actors were a **Community** activity, designed to solidify the **Legacy**.
*   Designing and distributing formats for proposals was a **Technology** and **Projects** task—creating the tools to gather the initial 'what we do'."

---

#### **Slide 5: Mapping the Process to HA - The Core Transdisciplinary Moment**
**(The slide now focuses heavily on the Community, Learning, and Technology dimensions, showing them as highly active.)**

**Presenter:**
"**Phase 3: Social Consultation** was the transdisciplinary heart of the project.

This was a massive **Community** engagement effort: workshops, interviews, online surveys with students, sessions in community centers. Every interaction was a touchpoint within the Community dimension.

But it was also a massive **Learning** process. The goal was to *learn* what the public needed and prioritized.

And this is where Human-AI collaboration became critical. The sheer volume of feedback—thousands of qualitative responses—was overwhelming. So, they leveraged **Technology**: Natural Language Processing and AI were used to process and analyze the global results of the consultation, extracting key ideas and public perceptions. This wasn't AI replacing human judgment; it was AI augmenting the **Learning** dimension, allowing the team to hear the voice of the **Community** at scale. This is a perfect example of transdisciplinary synergy."

---

#### **Slide 6: Mapping the Process to HA - Strategic Integration**
**(The slide now shows arrows converging from all dimensions towards the Legacy and Projects dimensions, with the "Technology" dimension highlighted as an enabler.)**

**Presenter:**
"**Phase 4: Strategic Integration** is where HA's structure prevents fragmentation.

*   All the data from **Community** and **Context** was consolidated in a central database, a core **Learning** and **Technology** asset.
*   Again, AI tools were used. Not just to process data, but to analyze historical trajectories of indicators and help define ambitious but realistic annual goals. This is **Technology** enabling better **Projects** aligned with the **Legacy**.
*   The validation sessions, where different government departments reviewed each other's content, was a crucial **Community** feedback loop, ensuring coherence.
*   And this culminated in the creation of 'Esperanza'—an AI chatbot. This agent, a part of the **Technology** dimension, was designed to make the final **Legacy** and **Projects** accessible to the entire **Community**."

#### **Slide 7: A Truly Transdisciplinary Outcome**
**(A summary slide showing how different fields contributed, mapped to the HA dimensions.)**

**Presenter:**
"Looking back, what made this process a transdisciplinary success?

It was the **shared framework**. Horizons Architecture provided the common ground.

*   **Social Scientists and public servants** led the **Community** engagement.
*   **Data Scientists and AI engineers** built the tools for the **Technology** and **Learning** dimensions.
*   **Government leaders** focused on defining the **Legacy**.
*   **Project managers** structured the **Projects**.

Everyone was working in their area of expertise, but they weren't in silos. They were contributing to a shared, dynamic model of the endeavor. The HA dimensions served as the common language, the integrating structure that allowed these different streams of knowledge to merge into a coherent, actionable Government Program. It managed the complexity by making the interconnections visible and actionable."

### What is TE



---

**TRANSDISCIPLINARY ENGINEERING**   

Today’s complex problems, especially those that have a large impact on the environment and society, require a holistic approach to solving them by considering knowledge from a diverse range of disciplines, technical as well as social, and from user and practitioner communities. Interchange of such cross-disciplinary knowledge is needed to determine the overall impact on the environment and society. Many examples exist of engineering innovations that have a large adverse effect on the environment by not considering measures and regulations for mitigating these effects, for example, non-biodegradable plastics, certain pesticides and fertilizing products, use of greenhouse gasses, the long-term effect of medication and medical procedures, etc.

To create engineering solutions that minimize environmental and societal damage, engineering design and practices must involve people with the necessary knowledge and methods to determine the impact of new products and services, not only on client and user satisfaction but also on long-term sustainability. Designers and engineers need to come from a wide range of disciplines to achieve such sustainable solutions. The support of advanced ICT technology is deemed indispensable.

[The International Society of Transdisciplinary Engineering (ISTE)](https://intsoctransde.org/) aims to create awareness that engineering practices need to evolve into transdisciplinary engineering practices in which the exchange of different types of knowledge and people from a diverse range of disciplines is fundamental.  

Transdisciplinary Engineering (TE) is the exchange of knowledge in the context of innovation, in product, process, organization, or social environment. Through ISTE, the exchange of knowledge is possible in its annual conferences, the TE Conferences, and through the publication of special issues in scientific journals.
