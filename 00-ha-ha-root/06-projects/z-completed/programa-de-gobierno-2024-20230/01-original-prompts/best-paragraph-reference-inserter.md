In academic writing, knowing when to reference an author directly, when a general idea does not require specific attribution, or when to paraphrase involves understanding the source of the information and how it's used in your text. Here’s a concise guide on how to approach each:

1. **Direct Reference**:
    
    - **When:** Use a direct reference when you are citing specific information, data, theories, or opinions that are unique to a particular source. This includes direct quotes or highly specific concepts that are clearly attributed to a particular author or work.
    - **How:** Include the author's name, publication year, and, if quoting, a page number. This should be formatted according to the citation style you are using (e.g., APA, MLA, Chicago).
2. **General Ideas**:
    
    - **When:** General knowledge or widely accepted facts do not require citations. These include things like historical dates, scientific facts that are common knowledge, or concepts that have no specific originator.
    - **Example:** "Photosynthesis in plants converts carbon dioxide and water into oxygen and glucose," is a general scientific fact and does not need citation.
3. **Paraphrasing**:
    
    - **When:** Paraphrasing involves restating someone else’s ideas or textual content in your own words. Even when paraphrasing, the underlying idea is derived from someone else's work, and thus requires proper citation to avoid plagiarism.
    - **How:** After restating the idea in your own words, include a citation to the original source. Unlike direct quotes, paraphrased content does not need quotation marks, but it does need to clearly convey your understanding of the source material without copying the original phrasing.

**Key Tips**:

- When in doubt, cite the source. It’s better to provide more citations than to risk plagiarism.
- Maintain your own voice and interpretation when paraphrasing; simply changing a few words is not sufficient.
- Use direct quotes sparingly to emphasize a point or when the original wording is particularly impactful or necessary for your argument.
